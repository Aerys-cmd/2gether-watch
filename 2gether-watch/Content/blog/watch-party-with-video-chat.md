A lot of watch-party tools stop at sync and text chat. That covers "watching the same thing at the same time," but it skips the part that actually makes it feel social — seeing and hearing the people you're watching with.

[Teleparty](/blog/teleparty-alternative), for example, has no calls or screen share at all — it's sync plus a chat sidebar. [2gether Watch](/) builds the call into the same room as the video.

## What's in the room

- **Video sync** — YouTube and direct video URLs, in lockstep across everyone present.
- **Live text chat** — for when a call isn't the vibe, or as a sidebar alongside one.
- **Audio/video calls** — optional webcam and microphone, toggled from the room's footer controls.
- **Screen sharing** — share your screen instead of a video link.

All of it runs in the same room, over the same peer-to-peer WebRTC connection — there's no separate call app to open alongside it.

## Why peer-to-peer matters specifically for calls

Video and audio are a different kind of data than a chat message — they're continuous, and they reveal a lot more about you than text does. Because 2gether Watch's calls are peer-to-peer, your camera and microphone feed travels directly to the other people in the room rather than through the server. The server's role is limited to signaling — helping peers find each other and set up the connection — not relaying the media itself.

## When you'd actually want this

- **[Long-distance movie nights](/blog/watch-movies-together-long-distance)** — seeing the other person's reactions in real time is most of the point.
- **Group watch parties** where half the fun is talking over the video.
- **Reaction content** — watching a trailer, a stream clip, or a video together and immediately talking about it, without switching to a different app to do so.

If the tool you're using now handles sync fine but leaves calls to a separate app, [try a room](/) that does both in one place.
